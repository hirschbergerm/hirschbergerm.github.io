In the summer of 2022 I was selected for an internship in NASA's Dusty Plasma Laboratory at Marshall Space Flight Center. I was tasked with developing a device that drops grains of lunar dust into a vacuum chamber where they are suspended by an electrodynamic balance in order to perform experiments.

Lunar dust is electrostatically charged due to ionization via solar wind and electron discharge via the sun's UV light.

In the Dusty Plasma Laboratory NASA is attempting to quantify the electrostatic charging properties of lunar dust via secondary electron yield experiments. To perform these experiments, samples of lunar dust recovered from the Apollo missions are charged and then dropped inside a vacuum chamber where an electrostatic "trap" sits. The trap is an assembly of precisely shaped electrodes that create a quadropole field which levitates the charged dust grains. Once a grain of dust is caught in the field, a UV light source bombards the dust grains, and the secondary electron yield can be measured.
